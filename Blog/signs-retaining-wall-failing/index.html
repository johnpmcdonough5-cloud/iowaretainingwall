7 Signs Your Retaining Wall Is Failing

Catch these seven warning signs early and you can often repair a wall before it becomes a full, costly rebuild.

Published July 14, 2026 · 6 min read

A retaining wall fails slowly, then all at once. The good news is that it almost always warns you first. If you catch the signs early, a repair is far cheaper than a rebuild — and much safer. Here are the seven red flags Eastern Iowa homeowners should watch for.

1. The wall is leaning or tilting

A wall that's tipping away from the slope it holds back is losing the battle with soil pressure. Even a slight lean means the wall's footing, drainage, or reinforcement is being overwhelmed. This is the most urgent sign.

2. Cracks are spreading

Hairline cracks can be cosmetic, but widening vertical or stair-step cracks signal structural movement. In poured concrete and brick walls especially, growing cracks mean water and frost are working the wall apart.

3. Bulging or bowing sections

When part of the wall pushes outward like a belly, water pressure has built up behind it. This is almost always a drainage failure — the number-one cause of retaining wall collapse in Iowa.

4. Water pooling or seeping through

Saturated soil is heavy soil. If you see water weeping through the wall face, pooling at the base, or eroding the ground behind it, the drainage system isn't doing its job. Learn why in our guide to retaining wall drainage.

5. Separating or crumbling blocks

Gaps opening between blocks or stones, or mortar crumbling out of joints, mean the wall is shifting. Iowa's freeze-thaw cycles accelerate this once water gets into the joints.

6. Soil erosion behind or beside the wall

If the ground above the wall is washing out or sinking, water is escaping past the wall instead of being managed by it. Left alone, this undermines the whole structure.

7. The wall was built without drainage or geogrid

Many older or DIY walls skip gravel backfill, drain pipe, and soil reinforcement. Even if it looks fine today, a wall built without these is living on borrowed time.

Can a failing retaining wall be repaired?

Sometimes. Minor leaning, isolated cracks, and drainage problems can often be corrected by adding drainage, re-grading, or reinforcing the wall. But once a wall is significantly bowed, tilted, or crumbling, a rebuild is usually safer and more cost-effective than repeated patches. The only way to know is an inspection — we offer free assessments across Eastern Iowa and will tell you honestly whether yours needs a repair or a rebuild.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a leaning retaining wall dangerous?

It can be. A leaning wall is under more soil and water pressure than it was designed to hold, and failure can happen suddenly. If a wall is tilting, keep people and vehicles away from both sides and have it inspected promptly.

How much does retaining wall repair cost?

Minor repairs like adding drainage or resetting a few blocks may run a few hundred to a couple thousand dollars. Major repairs approach the cost of a rebuild. A free on-site inspection is the best way to get an accurate number for your wall.

Why do retaining walls fail?

The leading cause is poor drainage. Without gravel backfill and a drain pipe, water builds up behind the wall and the pressure pushes it outward. Other causes include undersized footings, no soil reinforcement (geogrid), and building too tall without engineering.
